# Episode Retention Map

Use this reference when designing free episodes or early serialization flow. The goal is not to summarize plot. The goal is to assign each episode a retention job.

## Basic Rule

Early episodes must prove three things quickly:

- The genre fun is real.
- The protagonist can drive events.
- The story can repeat rewards without becoming flat.

## 1-5 Episode Rule

The first five episodes are the promise test.

| Episode | Job | Required Reward | Required Hook |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Prove premise and protagonist appeal | genre signal, immediate problem, protagonist action | "What happens next because of this impossible situation?" |
| 2 | Prove repeatability | first tactical win or discovery | new rule, new enemy, or new cost |
| 3 | Expand consequence | public reaction, relationship shift, or status change | someone notices or responds |
| 4 | Raise difficulty | stronger obstacle or moral/social cost | forced choice or reveal |
| 5 | Lock the reading habit | satisfying payoff plus larger gate | next arc entrance |

If episode 1 is mostly backstory, move backstory later and open with pressure.

If episodes 2-5 repeat the same beat, vary the reward type: status, knowledge, relationship, resource, threat, or public reaction.

## 1-10 Episode Rule

Episodes 6-10 prove the engine.

- Show that the protagonist's advantage creates new problems.
- Introduce a rival, institution, family, guild, company, academy, faction, or romance counterforce.
- Make the reader understand the next 20 episodes' direction.
- Pay off one early curiosity before opening a larger one.

Risk signs:

- The protagonist only reacts.
- The setting explains itself without forcing decisions.
- Rewards become invisible.
- The antagonist or love interest has no active move.

## 1-25 Episode Rule

Episodes 11-25 prepare paid conversion or long retention.

- Convert initial novelty into a larger objective.
- Attach personal stakes to external stakes.
- Build a delayed reward that feels close enough to wait for.
- Place at least one strong social reaction or relationship reversal.
- Prepare a free-final hook that does not feel arbitrary.

## Episode Function Menu

Assign one main function per episode:

- `premise proof`: demonstrate the concept in action.
- `skill proof`: show ability, talent, knowledge, or cheat working.
- `cost proof`: show the price of the advantage.
- `status shift`: rank, reputation, wealth, access, or public opinion changes.
- `relationship shift`: ally, rival, romance, family, or enemy relation changes.
- `world expansion`: a larger organization, rule, gate, region, or hierarchy appears.
- `mystery answer`: one question is answered.
- `mystery expansion`: the answer opens a larger question.
- `catharsis`: punishment, victory, rescue, humiliation reversal, or emotional payoff.
- `conversion pressure`: the next episode promises a high-value payoff.

## Retention Rhythm

Use a rhythm of payoff and pressure:

- Episode A: give reward, open cost.
- Episode B: explore cost, give tactical win.
- Episode C: reveal larger consequence.
- Episode D: force choice.
- Episode E: pay off choice, open next arena.

Avoid five consecutive episodes of only escalation. Readers need proof that waiting pays.

## Output Template

For each episode, produce:

- episode number
- main function
- opening pressure
- reader reward
- new pressure
- ending hook pattern
- next-click question
- comment trigger
- drop-off risk
- revision note
